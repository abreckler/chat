Surfacing the right information & incentives to crowd-source job referrals

“Ninety-nine percent of AI currently being developed is about replacing and ultimately exceeding human intelligence. Ours is different. It’s about amplifying human intelligence.” — Louise Rosenberg

At Xpo.Network, when we started examining the problem of matching global talent to the right opportunities (our mission), there was a strong temptation to ‘apply machine learning’ to come up with a new solution for identifying the right talent and job matches.

What originally looked like an information-filtering and matching problem due to the complex nature of the job search process, soon turned out instead to be an information-retrieval and availability problem. After coming to the realization that most of the answers about top talent, were silo-ed in private real-world social networks. Questions such as:

Who is good? Who is looking for a new opportunity?

What follows is an account of our proposed solution to this problem.

Incentive-systems vs Information-systems

When incentives can completely govern a system without intermediaries, as is the case with Bitcoin, they can achieve amazing things in terms of scalability without the need for intermediaries.

However, where this logic tends to breakdown is where direct incentives play a weaker role, either because they are too difficult to ‘get right’ in a complex system, such as an economy, or even at a more micro-level e.g: job search.

“That capitalism is chiefly a knowledge system, rather than an incentive system” — George Gilder (source)

Information Asymmetries in the Job Market

Coming back to the job-search problem we described earlier:

Most job applicants are unlikely to know a current employee at companies they are considering joining. And even if they did, it’s unlikely they could trust them to tell them the unvarnished truth. Employees have little incentive to say negative things about their employer unless very close with the person asking.

Similarly, employee’s first-degree networks may only get them so far in discovering the vast set of opportunities out there connected to their 2nd, 3rd, not to mention N’th degree connections.

This sparse commons is a classic case of natural market failure where relevant information is hard to come by, namely questions like:

  1. Which of my contacts are open to new job opportunities?
  2. Which of my loose contacts is currently actively looking for a job?
  3. Which kinds of jobs are they looking for?
  4. Who is currently hiring in my network and for what roles?

Our goal:

To design a system that that is able to solve for the information asymetries in the job search process, while maintaining a trusted and privacy-centric experience for the job seeker.

Layering in Incentives

Even though we’ve decided that financial incentives aren’t the holy grail for helping us to solve the problem of matching job seekers with jobs, it doesn’t mean we should do away with them entirely.

Trusted referrals are nothing new to the world of job matching. In fact, they are agreed to be one of the best ways to find new employees amongst employers and employees can benefit from them as well for making direct referrals.

The problem is that currently, there aren’t strong incentives or signaling mechanisms for mutual connections on LinkedIn to refer former colleagues to new jobs, because of the lack of information/signals in the market for talent.

In-direct Referrals & Recursive rewards change this equation

By attaching a “recursive” algorithm to our reward function, we are able to allow employees to tap into their respective personal networks to surface the right connections for the job, all without requiring a direct connection.

These sorts of referral bonuses are traditionally used for first degree referrals but haven’t been applied to the 2nd, 3rd and N’th degree referrals we are talking about.

The Darpa “Red Baloons Study” (diagram to the left) gives a clear example of how the MIT team was able to win the competition by leveraging a recursive incentive structure.

Our Solution

  1. Step one — Apply to join an Xpo.Network talent registry, you must first apply an demonstrate your competence in the relevant skill. This ensures that we only trust referrals from domain experts.
  2. Step two — Add endorsements. We recommend hires for companies based only on the network of endorsements. We don’t ask for your work history or resume (though you can add it optionally).
  3. Private & Spam-proof — you only see companies that should hire you on your jobs opportunities page. Companies only see you if you indicate interest in them first. Importantly: Your profile is not searchable.
  4. Monetize your network —because half of every recruiter fee is distributed among the chain of people whose invitations contributed to the eventually hire, you’ll be able to directly reap the rewards of your trusted network.

An Example

We charge 20% of the referred employee’s first-year salary from the employer and pass along 50% of this referral fee to the chain of referrers.

If a chain of four people leads to a $100k hire, each person in the chain involved receives $2,500 ($10k / 4 = $2.5k)

This ensures that everyone who contributed a connection to the ‘chain’ of referrers that led to a new hire is getting rewarded.

To prevent against people spamming the network and trying to maximize their potential payouts, we institute a ‘hard-cap’ of 10 recommendations to limit your connections to those that you know and trust.

We also leverage peer-verifications of new accounts via Twitter to guard against Sybil-attacks.

Conclusion

Leveraging real-world social networks for job referrals is hardly a new concept, however, as LinkedIn and Facebook have connected the professional and social graphs, the signal from each connection is able to transmit has weakened to the point where it no longer conveys meaningful information.

With Xpo, we attempt to bring trusted connections back to the forefront and help people better leverage their network’s to discover new opportunities.

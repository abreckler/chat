Are cards the past, present or future of information design?

Depending on whom you ask, ‘cards’ are either:

a) a new design pattern for laying out content in a container.

b) an old way to navigate to and from related information.

c) an old collectible item or way to play a game.

d) a new paradigm for the way content will be consumed on the web.

For the remainder of this article, we will be using the definition proposed by (d).

Why Cards?

Using cards as a method of displaying and inter-linking content is not new.

Hypercard was a program for early mac users that allowed for creative ‘stacking’ of content linked together to form stand-alone applications.

Some early killer apps included games like ‘myst’ and the whole earth catalog.

Today cards are bing used in all sorts of places. The most notable is by twitter, for publishers and advertisers to better format their content to make it more engaging and actionable.

Why Now?

If the predominant medium of our time is set to be the portable screen (think phones and tablets), then the predominant design pattern is set to be cards. The signs are already here… — @padday
Cards are emerging as the design pattern of choice for mobile devices which are centered around a feed of content.

Gone are the days of optimizing content for screens with 10 blue links.

In the words of BuzzFeed Founder and CEO, Jonah Peretti:

“Because so many publishers build their businesses on banner ads, they have to get people back to their site to make money…You should be using this distribution channel to show them content, not just a pointer to some other space.” — Jonah Peretti, TechCrunch

Cards as Design

Cards are first and foremost a design pattern.

This presentation by Chris Tse illustrates the different card design patterns broken down into 3 categories, ‘context’, ‘triggers’ and ‘lens’.

Google has it’s own set of criteria for card design, Twitter has it’s own too.

Cards as Data

Cards are first and foremost visual.

However, they are also backed by a powerful open protocol for meta-data tagging called ‘open graph’, created by Facebook.

This capability allows for cards to be transported across platforms while easily carrying their meta-data with them.

One possible future exists where a standard card protocol is established where every platform adopts a standard format for cards comprised of ‘title’ ‘link’ ‘featured image’ ‘description’ ‘author’ ‘timestamp’, etc.

One possible future scenarios is where platforms like facebook and twitter decide to support each other’s card formats to allow for cards to move even more seamlessly across platforms.

A more likely scenario is that there will be multiple similar, but non-standardized card formats which will leave developers and publishers of content scrambling to integrate with.

Cards, Cards, everywhere?

While cards are great at many things, they are not the end-all be-all building block for every container bet it a ‘board’, ‘stack’, ‘feed’ or traditional ‘page’.

Design

Not every kind of information or layout lends itself to being displayed as a card, as you can see: Google’s Material design principles: “Cards usage”

Data

Cards still aren’t evolved yet enough to be able to share their data & context across platforms in a standardized way as REST api’s for example.

This will likely evolve over time.

All in all, cards are an excellent way to both design and share information across platforms and while they have their limitations, are quickly emerging as a preferred design language of choice for mobile platforms in particular.

In conclusion:

Cards are the past, present AND future of content.

The Case for Incremental Innovations

Un-locking moments of non-consumption to go exponential.

No-one disagrees that it’s the big bold exponential thinking that is a necessary ingredient in building products and services that aim to eventually serve billions of people…however, what’s more actionable in the early days is figuring out how to unlock the ‘incremental’ new unit of consumption onto a network where new products and services can really begin scaling up.

Only in Silicon Valley can you be accused of thinking too ‘Incrementally’ and not ‘Exponentially’ enough.

It turns out that the breakthrough products and services of our time, succeed in growing, not by scrutinizing the behavior of their competition or existing consumption patterns or trying to invent completely novel ones, but instead by scrutinizing what Clay Christensen dubs ‘non-consumption’.

“You buy a house or move into a new apartment and there’s this big wall of unadorned space. So you buy a piece of art to stick there,”
“For the first three weeks, you notice it every time you walk past it — you enjoy it. But after that it becomes so commonplace that you don’t even notice it on the wall. What you’ve done is: you’ve spent money and brought a product home and consumed art for three weeks. Then you stop consuming that art — and what makes it worse is that this piece of art preempts the consumption of art in that space.
So I have a student who is building a business around putting a flat-screen, high-definition television screen on that wall space, with a lovely frame around it. And every three weeks, over the Internet, they will send you a fresh piece of art, so you can start consuming art again.” — Clayton Christensen, Harvard Magazine

Here are three examples over the last five years of world-changing companies who’s innovations could hardly be described as ‘exponential’ or ‘breakthrough’ at the time, which were able to successfully turn the ‘non-consumption’ event into an incremental consumption event of their product or service.

1) Uber: unlocking the incremental ride that wasn’t previously taken via any transportation system.

While there has been lots of noise around how Uber is devouring the old Yellow-cab business, the real disruption occurring is how they are bringing the previously un-taken ride online. By lowering the cost and improving the speed of their transportation product, they were able to bring onto their app the various incremental uses for transportation not currently being served by the incumbent taxis, but even the next best substitute (e.g: renting a car when traveling or walking or riding your bike to work) and the night out on the town not taken due to finding a friend who would drive.

The key enablers: Time (to get a ride) and cost.

2) Instagram: unlocking the incremental photo shared via the smartphone.

All the way back in 2010 when Instagram first launched, it wasn’t a common thing to share a photo (many megabytes) from a smartphone over a (painfully slow) 3g network. As arcane as it seems in hindsight, this was a real barrier to other mobile photo sharing apps that came along prior to Instagram.

The second ‘incremental’ improvement that Instagram pioneered was its use of filters to allow users to beatify their photos to the point where they felt they looked good enough to share and show off their creativity. These photos likely sat in users camera rolls previously ‘un-shared’.

The key enablers: Speed and filters.

3) SnapChat: unlocking the incremental casual photo exchange that wasn’t made because of lack of privacy and permanence.

By lowering the friction and anxiety behind sharing a photo of a casual moment, SnapChat was able to tap into a latent sharing behavior that users wanted to participate in, but hadn’t been comfortable doing previously via any existing photo-sharing apps.

The key enablers: The combination of the disappearing photo and the screenshot detection alert, which gave a strong sense of safety to a user looking to share a private or casual photo.

None of these products or services were successful in scaling up exponentially either because they only were able to grab share from an existing player or create an entirely new consumption experience.

Rather, they were successful because they were able to lower the friction in bringing that incremental consumption event onto their service that wasn’t happening anywhere previously.
